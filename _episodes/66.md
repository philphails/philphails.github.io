# What Respect, Intent, and Speaking Truth Really Mean in Relationships

There’s a moment in every hard conversation where you feel the pull — the desire to protect yourself, to be right, or to walk away. Greg Stephens has lived those moments. In this wide-ranging conversation on *Phil Phails*, he shares what it looks like to clean up incomplete relationships, face childhood wounds, and choose connection even when it feels impossible.

Greg’s work as a relationship coach, mediator, and author of *Build New Bridges* comes from lived experience. What he teaches isn’t theory. It’s the result of years of doing the uncomfortable work himself.

## The Haunted House We All Carry

Many of us grow up in homes that continue to haunt us long after we leave. Greg describes his childhood with a father who swung from one extreme to another — from hell-raiser to strict Southern Baptist preacher. The stories of burning books and records, disowning family members, and rigid rules paint a picture of a household shaped by fear and control.

Yet Greg didn’t stay stuck. He recognized the cycle of violence many families repeat and made a deliberate choice to break it. One of the most powerful turning points he shares is the moment he stopped spanking his daughter and chose connection instead.

That decision marked the beginning of a deeper awakening — moving from reaction to conscious choice, from repeating old patterns to intentionally building new ones.

## The Hardest Conversation: Facing His Father

The centerpiece of this episode is Greg’s account of repairing his relationship with his father. After cleaning up dozens of other incomplete relationships, he saved the hardest one for last. His mother warned him it might cost him everything.

What followed was a raw, multi-day process of honesty, ownership, and vulnerability. Greg owned his hypocrisy. His father expressed deep disappointment. In the tension, something shifted. They moved from confrontation to connection, eventually building a real father-son relationship in the years before his father’s passing.

This story isn’t a fairytale resolution. It’s a testament to what becomes possible when we stay in the room and choose curiosity over being right.

## Intent Versus Impact: The Communication Gap

A recurring theme throughout the conversation is the difference between what we mean and how we’re received. Greg emphasizes that while we’re responsible for our intent, we also have a responsibility to pay attention to impact.

He shares practical techniques: reflective listening, asking “How did that land for you?”, and putting a mirror to behavior without judgment. These tools help de-escalate conflict and rebuild trust.

Respect, he explains, isn’t abstract. It requires explicit conversations about what respect looks and feels like to each person. Without those conversations, we often project our own definitions onto others and feel hurt when they don’t match.

## Speaking Truth Respectfully

Learning to speak truth without destroying connection is a skill. Greg talks about practicing in low-stakes situations — like addressing people cutting in line — to build the muscle for higher-stakes moments.

He stresses the importance of private feedback, owning your part first, and staying grounded in safety and curiosity. The goal isn’t winning the argument. It’s restoring relationship.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Define respect explicitly** with the important people in your life. Ask what respect looks and feels like to them.
- **Pay attention to impact**, not just intent. Check in with others about how your words land.
- **Own your part first** in conflict. This often opens the door for the other person to do the same.
- **Practice in small moments**. Build the skill of speaking truth respectfully in everyday situations.
- **Choose curiosity over judgment**. Seeing others as allies, even when it’s hard, changes the energy of the interaction.
- **Stay in the room**. The willingness to stay present through discomfort is where real repair happens.

## Personal Reflection

Listening to Greg reminded me how much courage it takes to face our most difficult relationships. His stories aren’t polished success narratives — they’re honest accounts of messiness, fear, anger, and ultimately growth.

What stayed with me most is the idea that we don’t have to stay trapped in the haunted houses of our past. We can choose to stop repeating old cycles. It requires work, honesty, and a willingness to feel uncomfortable, but the freedom on the other side is real.
